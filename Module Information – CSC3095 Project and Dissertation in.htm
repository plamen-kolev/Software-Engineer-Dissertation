          CSC3095 Project and Dissertation in Computer Science / Information Systems

          Module leaders: Marie Devlin, Lindsay Marshall

          Information about Stage 3 Projects for 2016-17 will be summarised here. See coursework outlines in Ness for more details.

          Selecting a Project

          At the end of last academic year you should have attended the Dissertation Fair to talk to staff about possible projects and sign up for a theme.

          The themes and theme leaders are as follows:

          • Mobile - Lindsay Marshall
          • Web - Lindsay Marshall
          • Distributed systems / Cloud - - Paul Ezhilchelvan
          • Game engineering - Graham Morgan/Gary Ushaw
          • Bio-computing - Phil Lord
          • Security - Feng Hao
          • Software Engineering - Steve Riddle 
          • HCI - John Vines
          • Cyber -Physical Systems - John Fitzgerald
          • Teaching and learning - Marie Devlin
          • Algorithms - Jason Steggles
          •  Choose your own Project - Matt Collison

          If you have not already been allocated a supervisor by the theme leader by the end of Week 1, then you should contact the theme leader to sort this out.

          Once you have a project and a supervisor, the supervisor will use NESS to sign you up for that project.

          Many projects are supported by research groups. This means that the theme leader may pass you on to another staff member of the research group to balance the supervision load.

          Project components

           

          1. Lectures

          The following is the draft schedule of project lectures. PDF of the notes will be available from Blackboard after the lecture has been delivered.

            1. Doing a project 
            2. Project planning, Aims and Objectives 
            3.  Information skills 
            4. Giving presentations 
            5. Ethics 
            6. Technical Writing 

          Reading week: project presentatio

                    7. Research design /Questionnaires

           Semester 2 contains only one lecture

                    8. Poster/Demo/Dissertation

          3. Ethical approval (pass/fail: approved by module leaders)

           You will need to complete an ethical approval form with the help of your supervisor, to allow us to identify any ethical issues with the project (for example, any studies which involve users, collecting data about people). The completed form is to be submitted via NESS. All students MUST complete the preliminary ethical approval form. Failure to do so will mean you fail the module.

          4. Project presentation (5%)

          Presentations will take place in reading week,  Submit your slides in NESS by Friday 28th October 2016 and book a presentation slot in NESS. Slots will be made available shortly on NESS and we will notify you about this.
          For more information on what is required, see the Presentation Guidance.

          5. Project proposal (5%)

          Due 23:45 on 2nd December. The project proposal is a document of up to 4 pages (excluding references) in which you should describe:

          1. the aims and objectives of your project;
          2. a description of your project;
          3. prior work in the field (correctly cited and referenced);
          4. your plan for the second semester.

          Detailed information about what the project proposal should contain and how it is going to be marked can be found in the Marking Guidelines document.

          Semester 2 deliverables

          6. Poster (5%)

          Submit your poster to NESS by 17:00, Monday 20th March 2017. Posters from previous years’ students can be seen on display in the corridors in Claremont Tower. You need to design your poster and submit it to NESS: the School will get a physical copy printed.

          Marks will be given for:

          • technical content (scale of 1-5)
          • visual impact (scale of 1-5)

          You must include name, student number and project title. Aim to convey the essence of your project for a non-expert, in an attractive, informative way.  Don't paste large chunks of text from your dissertation chapters. Use images, figures, tables, short sentences, clustering of ideas to tell the reader:

          • What was the project's aim?
          • Why was this worth doing?
          • What did you actually do, and how?
          • What makes the result so interesting?
          • What further work could lead on from this?

          Basic requirements:

          • A1 (594 x 841 mm) Portrait format;
          • submit electronic copy to NESS (as JPG or PDF file) 
          • you can use Powerpoint or other drawing packages than can produce a file in .jpg or .pdf format;
          • font sizes: titles should be about 72-120pt, no text smaller than 20pt. Stick to a consistent font style

          7. Demonstration (5%)

          Demonstrations will take place in the Lindisfarne Room, Kings Road Centre on Wednesday 10th May 2017 between 1pm and 5pm. This event will give you a chance to show your supervisor and 2nd marker what you have done (and to answer questions). Students will be allocated a session to attend between 1pm and 5pm. You will have a stand on which you can display your poster and a table on which you can place your laptop and demonstrate your software.

          8. Dissertation (80%)

          Your dissertation is due 23:45  5th May 2017. You are required to submit a PDF copy to NESS  You may submit supplementary material, such as source code, to NESS in a separate submission -- see Supplementary Material (OPTIONAL) exercise in NESS. You are NOT required to submit a hard copy of your dissertation. 

          The final dissertation should include the following elements:

          1. Introduction: scene setting, the wider context of the problem you are addressing, aims/objectives should be elaborated in detail; outline of the rest of the dissertation (what material is where, and why).
          2. Background: state-of-the-art for the problem you are exploring
          3. What you did and why you did it: a high-level explanation rather than low-level implementation details (unless appropriate); cover software engineering aspects (design, implementation, testing strategies) or investigations, experiments as appropriate for your project.
          4. Results and Evaluation: the main outputs of your project, evaluation of the results obtained and of the project (e.g. your plans and engineering approach).
          5. Conclusions: how well did you meet the original objectives (and why didn't you meet them all?); both positive and negative conclusions; retrospective look back at what you have learned; suggestions for future, follow-on work.
          6. References.
          7. Appendices: test results (in detail), transcripts of interviews, fragments of code (referred to in the main body of the dissertation), a user manual (if appropriate).

          The dissertation should be up to 15,000 words, excluding references and appendices. Remember that this is a formal document to satisfy the examiners that you have carried out a substantial piece of work, investigated a solution to a problem, engineered an effective solution to that problem and learnt something during your time at Newcastle.

          The dissertation will take time to write, and you will need to give your supervisor time to read draft chapters.
