Dr CJ Gamble

Categories

Co-modelling and Simulation, Faults and Fault-tolerance, Autonomous Systems

The projects proposed are all related to using simulations to aid engineers when designing cyber-physical systems (CPS) using a "co-modelling" environment. Co-modelling is an approach which acknowledges that just using a single modelling tool and paradigm, or using multiples tools and paradigms separately, is not ideal for building confidence in the device being designed before a first prototype is constructed. In our co-modelling we use two tools, Overture and 20-sim. Overture allows the modelling of detailed control software using the language VDM-RT in discrete time, while 20-sim is aimed at modelling physical and electronic systems in continuous time. The projects below should be seen as suggested subjects and you should feel very welcome to come and discuss any ideas you might be interested in following in this area.

Dr RJ Payne

Categories

Systems of systems, cyber physical systems, architectural modelling, dynamic reconfiguration, formal modelling

I will supervise projects in which you will work with Newcastle-based researchers on leading international projects developing new tools and methods for engineering "cyber physical systems" (CPSs) and "systems of systems" (SoSs). These fields of research are the focus of the Cyber Physical Lab in Newcastle. CPSs are systems with both computing (cyber) and electrical/mechanical/etc (physical) elements. They may range from embedded controllers to network-connected systems. An SoS is a system built from components, each of which is an independent system in its own right, but which contributes towards the overall SoS. Examples include emergency response networks, power grids, digital content delivery and traffic management. Note that you do not need to have studied CPS or SoS before, but some reading about the subject will be required as part of the project. Several projects are available in this area.

Dr P Zuliani

Categories

Verification, model checking, systems biology, synthetic biology, logic, stochastic systems

My main research interest is developing verification technologies to ensure that complex computing systems meet their intended behaviour in terms of reliability, safety, accuracy and robustness. Computing systems are often used in safety-critical applications, where a malfunctioning can result in threats to, or even loss of, human life. For example, modern aircraft are flown most of the time by a computer program, while the pilot's commands are filtered by another computer to ensure that aircraft do not fly outside their performance envelope. Electronic biomedical devices (e.g., digital infusion pumps that deliver drugs or nutrients to patients) enjoy widespread use, as they offer superior flexibility and accuracy with respect to traditional devices.

Can we verify such complex systems using currently available verification technologies? Unfortunately, the answer is “no”. The staggering complexity of the systems often results in astronomically large state spaces, making exhaustive-search techniques infeasible. We need a more scalable approach that enables us to verify the systems we can build today and those we will build in the future. This need applies to traditional engineering applications such as cyber-physical systems, as well as to novel applications such as synthetic and systems biology.

The projects listed below may feature programming work or theoretical work, depending on your preference. Also, you are welcome to propose *YOUR OWN* project in any of my interest areas above - feel free to contact me paolo.zuliani@ncl.ac.uk
